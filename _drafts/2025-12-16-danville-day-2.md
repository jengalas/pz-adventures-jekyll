We all slept well, and we were up by 5:45am to make sure we were ready and at the hospital in plenty of time for the surgery. Lucy and I had a quick breakfast at the hotel restaurant (granola parfait for her and oatmeal with craisins and granola for me, sadly nothing for Dad) before catching the shuttle for the 5-minute ride to the hospital.

As usual, we were significantly early to check in at the surgery area. Then we began to wait. At first, everything seemed to be moving along smoothly as usual: my father had his vitals taken, and I checked in with the "concierge" to make sure they had my contact information and knew that Lucy and I would be waiting for updates via text message. Soon my father went back to pre-op.

And we waited, and waited, and waited. My father's status on the board wasn't changing. By 10:00am, Lucy decided to go for a walk to stretch her legs, and then I did the same after she came back, because my father was still in pre-op. We were getting concerned but not specifically about my father; there just seemed to be an overall backup of procedures.

My walk ended up being more than a short stroll. I had been intrigued by the "Geisinger Trails" on the mountain above the hospital since the first time I saw them on the map, and this seemed like a good opportunity to at least walk to the trailhead to see what the area was like. The trailhead was just about exactly 1 kilometer from the hospital entrance, so I figured it would give me a good long walk but one that I could still complete quickly, if Lucy were to message me and say that things were finally moving along.

But she didn't (actually, she did message to say that nothing was happening and to "keep walking"), so I did. I had to - the trails were (or had been, at one time) named after snakes!

I'm not sure what the story was here, but Google Maps and the maps on my Garmin watch called one of the trails the Rattlesnake Trail and the Copperhead Trail respectively. Maybe one was correct and one wasn't, or the trail had different snake names at different points in time. Now, the trails here all seem to be named after medical terminology. But I liked the snake references so much that I decided to hike up into the hills and try to make a short loop.

The air temperature was cold (low teens), but it was sunny and not very windy, so the conditions in the woods were really pleasant. I was hiking quickly, not knowing when I would need to return to the hospital, and my rapid pace combined with the steep trail and snow cover meant that I warmed up very quickly.
